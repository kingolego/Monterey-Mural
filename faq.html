  2. “When was the Monterey Mural created?”

    The Mural was conceived in 1983, one of a series of art acquisitions for the Conference Center, led by Peter Coniglio, the mayor of Monterey during the initial Center's development. Maestro Granizo worked on the Mural through 1983 and 1984 — see his dates and initials in the bottom row of tiles. During this time he also created the larger Olympic Fantasy in Los Angeles.

    At the Mural's unveiling on March 27, 1984, Granizo was glad his work was able to include “every type of person: the carefree Indians, merchants, photographers and honky-tonk vendors.” Granizo dedicated his mural to “people I relate to, who don't wear shoes or comb their hair ... to the physically disabled [his left leg is paralyzed — an injury incurred on Utah Beach during the WWII D-Day assault] ... to the Spanish-speaking ... to your citizens ... (and) to people who enjoy life.”

  3. “What's missing from the Mural?” or “What places should we visit that aren't depicted?”

    Now one of the area's major attractions, the Aquarium opened in 1984 — the same year as the Mural.
    Ed “Doc” Ricketts (1897-1948) and his Pacific Biological Laboratories on Cannery Row. His BFF, John Steinbeck, has a cameo in the mural (A60). Tours of “Doc's Lab” will hopefully start again soon. If only someone had suggested to Granizo to christen one of the boats in the harbor as the Western Flyer — Ricketts and Steinbeck's chartered 1940 voyage on the Flyer to the Gulf of California was popularized in their books.

  4. “Is this art best described as a tile mural or a mosaic?”

    Why choose? Both terms alliterate with “Monterey”!

  5. “How much did the Mural cost?”

    Sources have quoted the cost as either $72,000 or $77,000. A member of the Conference Center Arts Commission said in 1984, “you couldn't tile your shower for this cost [by the square foot].”

  6. “How many scenes or subjects are represented in the Mural?”

    We don't know. Granizo proudly forswore sketches, planning or other design documentation of his work. As Dennis Copeland remarks, “So many people and events colorfully depicted that some are hidden in plain sight, some deliberately by the artist.” There have been claims of 150 scenes and we hope to document them all here.
