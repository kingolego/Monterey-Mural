The Monterey Mural

Synopsis

This colorful tile mural, depicting the history of Monterey, California, was created by Guillermo Wagner Granizo. The mural is outdoors on the west wall of the Monterey Conference Center along Pacific Street and features dozens of scenes from hundreds of years of equally colorful history in (Alta) California’s first capital.

Beginning with the culture of the Rumsen/Ohlone first nations and the exploration of our Pacific coast by Europeans, the Californian muralist Wagner Granizo displays a dazzling palette of colors and equally impressive knowledge of local history. From the founding of Monterey in 1770 by Portolá and Serra to the Gold Rush, the mural captures major events and movers/shakers from the Spanish, Mexican and early American eras. The 20th Century scenes are populated with the likes of Crosby, Morse and Steinbeck and all that Monterey has to offer, at least up until its unveiling by the City of Monterey in 1984!

Thirty years later -- while the Conference Center was being renovated -- the tiles were restored and then reinstalled in 2018 at its original location near the northeast corner of Del Monte Avenue & Pacific Street in downtown Monterey.

About maestro Guillermo Wagner Granizo

Before his death in 1995, Wagner Granizo was working on an autobiography, including a series of 105 new (small) murals illustrating his life. The artist's sons, Ron and Bob (also a muralist), have recently completed and posthumously published this illustrated autobiography on Amazon. You can also see the entire autobiographical series and the panels' descriptions on his website, GranizoArt.com! (The Monterey Mural is depicted on one page; however, the book is not intended to be a catalog or retrospective of his entire lifework.) While brief, the first person reminiscences and dream explorations are enchanting and provide welcome insights into the life of this true artist. Much of the material here is based on this autobiography.

The Garfield Library at the University of Pennsylvania also hosts an on-line presentation of his life series here. Dr. Eugene Garfield was a patron of the arts and founder of the Institute for Scientific Information and The Scientist and commissioned one of Wagner Granizo's largest works, the Cathedral of Man, prominently displayed at ISI headquarters in Philadelphia.

Wikipedia currently lists less than a dozen major public works. The artist believed he created over 7,000 murals, producing 500 murals/year during the height of his career in the 1980s. If he didn't have a paying commission, he would still create and donate the new work to children's hospitals. Most of his work is not as large as Olympic Fantasy or Monterey Mural, 2232 and 495 ft², respectively, but many are larger than the panels in the life series, each 9 ft².
